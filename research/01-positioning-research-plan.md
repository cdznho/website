# Research plan: Does the enterprise positioning help or hurt individual-learner conversion?

**Study owner:** Cedric De Schaut
**Timeline:** 3–4 weeks
**Method:** Moderated 1:1 interviews + concept test
**Participants:** 8 individual SEO/marketing practitioners
**Last updated:** April 2026

---

## 1. Background and framing

The homepage of cedricdeschaut.com is positioned toward **enterprise teams** — the H1, trust bar, featured-course card, and final CTA all use enterprise language ("enterprise teams," "multinationals," "large teams," "team training"). However, the only immediately purchasable product is a **$49 self-serve course** — a price point and purchase flow aimed at individuals.

This creates a positioning tension. The critical research question is:

> **For the individual practitioner who lands on cedricdeschaut.com and could buy the $49 course, does the enterprise-first framing build trust ("this person trains Fortune 500s, so the course must be good") or create friction ("this isn't for me")?**

A secondary question:

> **What would make an individual learner confidently buy the $49 course within their first session on the site — and what, specifically, is stopping them today?**

## 2. Research objectives

1. Understand how individual SEO/marketing practitioners currently discover and evaluate AI SEO training.
2. Observe first-impression reactions to cedricdeschaut.com — what they think it is, who it's for, and whether they feel included or excluded.
3. Surface the specific trust signals, objections, and questions that drive or block a $49 purchase decision.
4. Test three positioning variants (enterprise-first, individual-first, hybrid) to find the framing that maximizes individual conversion without sacrificing enterprise credibility.
5. Produce a prioritized set of copy, design, and IA recommendations the site owner can ship within 30 days.

## 3. Participants: who we recruit

**N = 8 individual learners** (the sweet spot for thematic saturation in qualitative research — you'll see ~80% of themes by interview 5, the remaining 3 confirm).

### Recruitment criteria (screen for all)

- Works in SEO, content, or digital marketing — either in-house or freelance/agency
- Has personally paid for at least one online course or paid resource in the last 12 months (validates willingness to buy)
- Has used ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini at least weekly in their work
- **NOT** a decision-maker for enterprise training budgets >$5k (that's a different study)
- Has never heard of Cedric De Schaut (so we see cold first impressions)

### Segments to hit (aim for spread)

| Segment | Target n | Why |
|---|---|---|
| In-house SEO at a mid-market company (50–500 employees) | 3 | Most common visitor profile |
| Freelance / independent SEO consultant | 2 | High willingness to self-invest |
| Content marketer at a startup | 2 | Adjacent buyer, curious about AI SEO |
| Agency SEO practitioner | 1 | Cross-check |

### Incentive

$100 gift card per 45-minute session. Standard for B2B practitioners — lower and you'll get worse recruits or flakes.

## 4. Method

**Moderated 45-minute Zoom interviews with screen share.**

Each session has four parts:

1. **Context (10 min)** — How they learn, what they've bought, current AI SEO knowledge level
2. **First-impression site walk (15 min)** — Live walkthrough of cedricdeschaut.com, think-aloud
3. **Concept test (15 min)** — Three hero variants shown, reaction + preference
4. **Wrap (5 min)** — Open-ended "what would have made you buy today?"

## 5. Timeline

| Week | Activities |
|---|---|
| **Week 1** | Finalize guide, write screener, start recruiting via UserInterviews.com or Respondent.io, prep three hero concept variants |
| **Week 2** | Conduct interviews 1–4, daily debrief notes, iterate guide if needed after interview 3 |
| **Week 3** | Conduct interviews 5–8, begin affinity mapping |
| **Week 4** | Synthesis, deliver report + highlight reel + recommendations |

**Single biggest schedule risk:** recruiting 8 participants with all criteria in a week takes ~15–20 screens. Start recruiting on day 1.

## 6. Interview guide

### Part 1 — Context (10 min)

*Goal: understand their current learning behavior and AI SEO relationship. Don't mention the site yet.*

- Walk me through the last time you learned a new SEO or marketing skill. How did you decide what to learn and from whom?
- What was the last paid resource (course, book, tool, community) you bought for your work? What made you pull the trigger?
- When you think about "AI SEO" or "getting mentioned in ChatGPT," how confident are you that you know what good practice looks like? *(probe: 1–10, why?)*
- Who are the people or brands you trust most on AI SEO content right now? *(listen for names we should be cited alongside)*

### Part 2 — First-impression site walk (15 min)

*Goal: capture unprompted reactions. Do NOT lead.*

**Setup script:**
> "I'm going to share a URL in the chat. Please open it, and as you look at it, just think out loud — tell me what you're noticing, what you're thinking, any reactions. There's no right answer. I'll stay quiet."

Share `https://www.cedricdeschaut.com/` — let them explore for 2–3 minutes silently.

**Probes to use after silent exploration:**

1. In one sentence, what is this site?
2. Who do you think this is for?
3. *(Check inclusion/exclusion)* Do you feel like this site is speaking to you, or to someone else? Tell me more.
4. What did you notice first? What drew your eye?
5. If you wanted to learn AI SEO today, what would you do next on this page?
6. *(Point to the $49 course card)* What do you think of this? What questions would you need answered before you'd buy it?
7. Is there anything here that makes you trust this person more? Anything that makes you trust them less?
8. *(If they didn't spontaneously react to "enterprise" language)* The page uses the words "enterprise," "multinational," and "large teams" several times. What does that make you feel about whether this is for you?

### Part 3 — Concept test (15 min)

*Goal: see how three positioning variants perform on the same audience.*

Show three hero mockups side by side (static images or Figma frames):

**Variant A — Enterprise-first (current site)**
> AI SEO training for enterprise teams
> Help your team win visibility in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews — before your competitors do.
> [View the AI SEO course] [Book team training]

**Variant B — Individual-first**
> Get your brand cited in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews
> The AI SEO playbook used by SEO teams at 30+ multinationals — now available as a 2-hour self-paced course.
> [Buy the course — $49] [Team access →]

**Variant C — Hybrid / outcome-first**
> Learn the AI SEO system trusted by 30+ global enterprises
> Whether you're a practitioner levelling up or a team lead rolling out training — the same framework, sized to fit.
> [For individuals — $49] [For teams — Book a call]

**For each variant ask:**
- Who is this for?
- How much would you expect the product to cost?
- On a 1–10, how likely are you to click through and explore?
- *(After all three)* Which one makes you most likely to buy today, and why?

### Part 4 — Wrap (5 min)

- If I waved a magic wand and fixed one thing about this site to make you confidently buy the $49 course today, what would it be?
- Anything we didn't cover that you want to say?
- Thank them; confirm incentive logistics.

## 7. Analysis plan

### During fieldwork
- Take structured notes in a shared spreadsheet with columns: participant, segment, first-impression quote, trust signals mentioned, friction points, variant preference, top fix
- Debrief within 2 hours of each session — cold notes get lost

### After fieldwork (Week 4)

1. **Affinity mapping** — every observation onto a virtual sticky in FigJam/Mural, cluster into themes. Do this with one collaborator if possible; solo affinity is biased.
2. **Impact/effort matrix** — plot each recommended fix by *expected conversion impact* (low/med/high) and *engineering effort* (hours/days/weeks). Ship the top-right quadrant first.
3. **Jobs-to-be-done framing** — for each segment, write the "When I ___, I want to ___, so I can ___" statement. This becomes the positioning test for every future page.

## 8. Deliverables

- **Research report (8–12 pages)** — objectives, method, participants, findings organized by theme, recommendations ranked by impact/effort
- **Highlight reel (5–7 min video)** — stitched clips of the most telling moments, especially the "who is this for?" and "do you feel included?" answers. This is the artifact that actually changes minds.
- **One-page summary** — for stakeholders who won't read the report
- **Updated hero recommendation** — which variant to ship, with copy and layout spec ready for handoff

## 9. Risks and caveats

- **Selection bias:** People who agree to a 45-min interview are more patient than average. Your actual site visitors may bounce faster than interviewees.
- **Demand characteristics:** Participants often say they'd buy when they won't. Weight behavioral signals (what they clicked, how long they stayed on the course card) over stated intent.
- **The framing tension flagged at the top:** This study tells you whether the *current* enterprise positioning hurts individual conversion. It does NOT tell you whether the enterprise business is bigger than the individual business — that's a commercial question, not a research question, and the answer shapes whether these recommendations should even be shipped.
- **N = 8 is directional, not definitive.** If findings are unanimous (6+ of 8 say the same thing), act on them. If findings split 4/4, run a quantitative survey (n=200+) before shipping changes.

## 10. What this study does NOT do

Out of scope, worth flagging for a future study:

- Validating the *enterprise* buyer's reaction to the site (different study, different recruits)
- Testing pricing sensitivity on the $49 course (survey or A/B test, not interviews)
- SEO / AI-citation performance (analytics study, not user research)
- Conversion-rate A/B testing of the final recommended hero (ship, measure, iterate)
